What Science Really Says About Kids and Social Media

Hi friend,

If you’re a parent, grandparent, teacher, or anyone who cares about young people, you’ve probably seen the scary headlines: social media is destroying an entire generation. It’s a fear that hits close to home for most of us.

This week, I went deep into the research—hundreds of published studies—to find out what the science actually says. And the answer? It’s far more nuanced, and far more hopeful, than those headlines suggest.

Teens spend an average of 4.8 hours per day on social media, and yes, heavy use does correlate with mental health issues. But researchers analyzing the same data sometimes reach different conclusions. What seems to matter most isn’t how long kids are online, but what they’re doing there—and how the rest of their childhood is structured.

Key Takeaway: The most compelling framework is the “Rewired Childhood” hypothesis—it’s not just phones. Less free play, more helicopter parenting, constant performance pressure, and always-on connectivity are combining to create a perfect storm. The answer isn’t to panic or ban screens. It’s to rebuild the foundations: free play, media literacy, open conversation, and modeling the habits we want to see.
