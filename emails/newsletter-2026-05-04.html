Creativity is a wellness practice (the data is stunning)

Hey friend,

I want to confess something a little unfashionable. I used to think of creativity as a luxury — a thing to get to after the real work was done. The brand, the workouts, the inbox, the family logistics. Creativity could wait.

This week I went deep into the research and the conversation between Rick Rubin and Andrew Huberman, and I had to retire that whole frame. A 14-year longitudinal study in the BMJ found that older adults who engaged with creative arts even occasionally had a 14 percent lower risk of mortality. Frequent engagement: 31 percent lower. Cortisol drops within 45 minutes of creative absorption. Cognitive reserve builds. The nervous system regulates the same way it does in meditation.

Creativity isn’t the dessert. It’s the medicine.

This week’s takeaway: You are not the source of creativity. You are the antenna. The world is offering itself to you all the time — the work is to keep the antenna clean. That changes everything: creativity stops being about producing more and starts being about perceiving more. Perception, unlike productivity, is a wellness state.

The full article walks through the neuroscience (it’s a three-network whole-brain story, not a left-brain/right-brain myth), Rick Rubin’s reframe of source as something you tune into, and three practical, evidence-supported daily habits: the Sensory Walk, the Morning Page, and the Quiet Hour. It also explains why the kitchen is one of the easiest creative laboratories in your home.
