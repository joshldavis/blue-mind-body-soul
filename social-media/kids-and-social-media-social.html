Slide 2: THE NUMBERS: Teens spend an average of 4.8 hours/day on social media. Some spend 6-8 hours. That’s a LOT of waking life on screens.
Slide 3: YES, there are correlations between heavy social media use and depression, anxiety, and self-harm in teens. But here’s the catch…
Slide 4: CORRELATION ≠ CAUSATION. Researchers analyzing the same data sometimes reach DIFFERENT conclusions. The science is more nuanced than the headlines.
Slide 5: THE GOLDILOCKS EFFECT: Some research suggests a "sweet spot"—a little social media may be beneficial. Too much is where problems start. It depends on the PERSON.
Slide 6: WHAT matters more than HOW LONG: Passive scrolling harms more than active engagement. Video-chatting a friend ≠ doom-scrolling highlight reels.
Slide 7: THE BIGGER PICTURE: It’s not just phones. Less free play + more overparenting + constant connectivity = a "rewired childhood" that may be the real culprit.
Slide 8: BOYS STRUGGLE TOO. The conversation has focused on girls, but boys face rising depression, self-harm, and gaming addiction. Different patterns, same pain.
Slide 9: WHAT ACTUALLY HELPS: Delay social media for young kids. Teach media literacy. Protect free play. Create tech-free zones. Model healthy habits yourself.

Full Post with Discussion Question

As a psychologist and a parent, the headlines about kids and social media scare me too. "Phones are destroying a generation." "Social media is toxic for teens."

But when I actually sat down with 200+ published studies, the picture I found was far more nuanced—and more hopeful—than those headlines suggest.

Yes, teens spend an average of 4.8 hours a day on social media. Yes, heavy use correlates with depression and anxiety. But the research also shows that what kids DO online matters more than how long they’re there. And the biggest insight? Social media is just one piece of a larger puzzle—a "rewired childhood" that includes less free play, more helicopter parenting, and constant pressure to perform.

The answer isn’t to panic. It’s to understand—and then act thoughtfully.
