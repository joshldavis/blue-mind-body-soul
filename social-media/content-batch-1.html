They're also healthier, more resilient, and more engaged. Emotions aren't the enemy. Unconscious emotions are.
Slide 3
Strategy 1: Situation Selection Choose Your Battles Before you even encounter a difficult emotion, you can choose situations that won't trigger you. ✓ Limit exposure to energy-draining people ✓ Delegate frustrating tasks ✓ Work in environments that support you Prevention > management
Slide 4
Strategy 2: Situation Modification Change the Environment Can't avoid a situation? Change it. ✓ Break complex problems into chunks ✓ Ask for help ✓ Take regular breaks ✓ Change your workspace Make the situation itself less overwhelming.
Slide 5
Strategy 3: Attention Deployment Redirect Your Focus You can't always change circumstances, but you can change where you direct your attention. ✓ Take a 5-minute break ✓ Listen to music ✓ Look at photos that make you happy ✓ Step outside for fresh air Mental resets = emotional shifts
Slide 6
Strategy 4: Cognitive Change Reframe Your Thinking How you interpret a situation determines how you feel about it. Critical feedback = threat or opportunity? Mistake = failure or learning? Rejection = proof of worthlessness or information? Choose a perspective that's both true and useful.
Slide 7
Strategy 5: Response Modulation Manage Your Expression You feel frustrated. You choose to express composure. It's not bottling. It's choosing professional presence while you process feelings privately. Later: exercise, journaling, or conversation. In the moment: intentional expression.
Slide 8
Which Strategy is Right for YOU? 🦋 Social Chameleon → Situation Selection 🔧 Problem Solver → Situation Modification 🧠 Thinker → Attention Deployment ☀️ Optimist → Cognitive Change 💼 Professional → Response Modulation You naturally prefer some. But develop flexibility with all 5.

The Surprising Truth About Difficult Emotions You don't want to feel good all the time. Frustration signals you care about something. Anger motivates you to set boundaries. Main Post (with Discussion Question)

We've been lied to. For decades, the narrative has been: "Emotions have no place at work. Keep it professional. Don't let your feelings show." It's advice that's fundamentally backwards—and it's costing us our wellbeing. As a psychologist who has spent years studying what makes people thrive, I can tell you: the research confirms something different entirely. People who understand their emotions make better decisions. They solve problems more creatively. They maintain better relationships. They're healthier, more resilient, and more engaged in their work. Emotions aren't the enemy. The enemy is being unconscious about them—letting them control you rather than working with them skillfully. Here's what I want to share: emotion regulation is a learnable skill, not something you're born with. And there are 5 core strategies that work. SITUATION SELECTION Before you even encounter a difficult emotion, you can choose situations that won't trigger you. Limit exposure to energy-draining people. Delegate frustrating tasks. Work in environments that support you. Prevention is better than management. SITUATION MODIFICATION If you can't avoid a situation, change it. Break complex problems into chunks. Ask for help. Take regular breaks. Change your workspace. Make the situation itself less overwhelming. ATTENTION DEPLOYMENT You can't always change your circumstances, but you can change where you direct your attention. Take a 5-minute break. Listen to music. Look at photos that bring you joy. Step outside. These mental resets shift your emotional state. COGNITIVE CHANGE How you interpret a situation determines how you feel about it. Critical feedback can be a threat or an opportunity. A mistake can be failure or learning. A rejection can be proof of worthlessness or valuable information. Choose a perspective that's both true and useful. RESPONSE MODULATION Sometimes you feel something internally, but you choose to express it differently. You're frustrated in a meeting, but you maintain composure. Later, you process those feelings through exercise, conversation, or journaling. That's healthy emotional regulation. Most of us naturally gravitate toward certain strategies based on our personality. Some are natural "situation selectors." Others are problem-solvers who modify environments. Some are thinkers who need mental breaks. Some are optimists who reframe easily. Some are professionals who can keep it together under pressure. The key is understanding your natural tendency—and developing flexibility to use other strategies when needed. Here's what I've learned: emotion regulation doesn't happen in a vacuum. Your workplace culture, your boss's leadership style, and your team's dynamics all influence how well you can manage emotions. If you work in an environment where emotions are shamed, your struggle. But in a place where vulnerability is okay and support is offered, you thrive. If you're a leader: the emotional culture you create directly impacts your team's performance. People who feel emotionally safe take more risks, admit mistakes faster, and support each other better. That translates to better results across every metric that matters.
