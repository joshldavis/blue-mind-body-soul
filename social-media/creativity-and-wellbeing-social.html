Caption

Creativity isn't a talent reserved for artists. It's a wellness practice. And it might be the most underused longevity tool we have. A 14-year BMJ study followed older adults and found that occasional engagement with creative arts cut mortality risk by 14%. Frequent engagement? 31%. The mechanism is gorgeous: creative absorption lowers cortisol, builds cognitive reserve, regulates the nervous system, and feeds the kind of meaning-making that buffers against depression. But here's the part that finally clicked for me — Rick Rubin's reframe in The Creative Act. You are not the source of creativity. You are the antenna. The world is offering itself to you all the time. The work is to keep your antenna clean. That changes everything. Creativity stops being about producing and starts being about perceiving. And perception, unlike productivity, is a wellness state. Three practices that train this: 1. The Sensory Walk (15 min, no phone, one sense) 2. The Morning Page (3 pages by hand, no editing) 3. The Quiet Hour (one creative act per week, zero outcome) The kitchen counts. The garden counts. Rearranging a room counts. A creative life is not a productive life. It is a perceptive life. And it is available to you, today, in the next 10 minutes. Creativity stops being about producing things and starts being about perceiving deeply. And perception, unlike productivity, IS a wellness state.
